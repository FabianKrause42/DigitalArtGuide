We are delighted to present online since 1996, a major 
survey exhibition of Olia Lialina, offering a compre-
hensive insight into the oeuvre of the net art pioneer 
through numerous works created over the past 30 
years.
As part of the first generation of net artists, Lialina uti- 
lised the technical structures of the internet from the 
very beginning to create browser-based works that 
played a pivotal role in the 1990s and 2000s in estab- 
lishing an entirely new artistic medium and a new im-
materiality in art. 
Early on, she explored both the narrative possibilities 
of the web as well as the manifestations of an online 
culture that was only just beginning to emerge and 
develop its own structures and modes of existence. 
Like a digital archaeologist, she archives and pre-
serves the aesthetic potentials of these beginnings  
in numerous works and series.
Her works demonstrate that the web initially func-
tioned as a heterogeneous, democratic space that 
both challenged and enabled users’ creativity and 
individual expression. It was only its subsequent co- 
optation by commercial interests, coupled with the 
premise of “user-friendliness”, that the sleek inter-
faces characterising the internet as we know it today 
came to dominate.
Olia Lialina’s practice therefore consistently carries  
a media-critical dimension that reveals what usually 
remains hidden. Her works can thus also be under-
stood as an appeal to us users to empower ourselves

Best Effort Network, 2015/2020
Website
https://best.effort.network/
Courtesy: Garage Museum of Contemporary Art, 
Moskau
Best Effort Network visualizes the usually invisible processes of data trans-
mission on the internet. When visiting best.effort.network, users encounter a 
website displaying an animation of the artist endlessly spinning on a carousel 
against a grey gradient background. If she suddenly disappears, it means that 
another person has accessed the website and their request has been prior-
itized by the server. Since Olia cannot exist simultaneously in multiple browsers, 
she now appears on the other visitor’s screen. Only when the original request 
reaches the front of the queue again does she return.
The work is based on the principle of a “best-effort network,” which underlies 
the internet itself. In such a network, data packets are transmitted without guar-
antees regarding successful delivery, order, or speed. Their transmission may 
be delayed, packets may be lost, or they may need to be resent. Best Effort Net-
work makes these normally hidden processes visible and tangible. Visitors are 
welcome to access the link on their smartphones and try it out for themselves.

Lossless, 2018
Animated GIFs, LED panels
Approx. 160 × 1.000 cm 
Courtesy: The artist, Roehrs & Boetsch, Zurich
Lossless is an installation consisting of LED panels displaying animated GIFs 
from the website Blingee.com (2006–2024). The platform primarily catered 
to young users, allowing them to decorate images with glitter effects, blinking 
stickers, and personalized collages that could then be shared on social net-
works such as MySpace.
For Olia Lialina, Blingee.com is a striking example of “vernacular web aesthet-
ics” and digital folklore. Vernacular web aesthetics refers to the visual forms of 
expression shaped by users during the early internet era. Personalized web-
sites, GIFs, and the stickers and collages created on Blingee.com emerged 
outside professional design standards and spread through imitation and adap-
tation within online communities. As forms of digital folklore, they demonstrate 
how users actively shaped the culture and aesthetics of the internet.
The GIFs, often perceived as kitschy or amateurish, stand in contrast to the 
standardized and highly polished interfaces of today’s web. Through the frag-
mented arrangement of the LED panels, reminiscent of archaeological artifacts, 
Lossless presents these visual worlds as digital relics of a past internet culture 
and emphasizes the importance of preserving them as part of the history of the 
web. The elements exhibited come from the files uploaded by Blingee’s leg-
endary stamp maker Irina Kuleshova, whose archive is preserved by Olia Lialina.

The Whole Internet, 2018
Two books
Each 22.86 × 18.42 × 2.54 cm
The Whole Internet presents the first and last editions of The Whole Internet 
User’s Guide and Catalog as an interactive readymade. Written by Ed Krol and 
published by O’Reilly in 1992 and 1999, the book is considered one of the first 
popular introductions to the Internet, making its then little-known structures 
accessible to a broader audience. Its title and format were inspired by Stewart 
Brand’s magazine Whole Earth Catalog.
The Whole Earth Catalog was a countercultural magazine that, beginning in 
1968, compiled knowledge, tools, and resources for self-sufficiency, ecology, 
and independent learning. Owing to its principle of “Access to Tools” the cata-
logue is often regarded as an important precursor to the World Wide Web as it 
curated, reviewed, and linked information sources in a way that anticipated how 
people would later navigate the internet. Steve Jobs therefore described the 
Whole Earth Catalog as “a sort of Google in paperback form, 35 years before 
Google came along”.
By juxtaposing the first and final editions of The Whole Internet User’s Guide 
and Catalog, Olia Lialina’s work highlights the rapid transformation of the World 
Wide Web and its cultural perception — from a specialized network to an om-
nipresent medium. Visitors are encouraged to browse and read the books, im-
mersing themselves in the world of The Whole Internet User’s Guide and Cat-
alog.

Web Pages That Suck / Son of Web Pages  
That Suck, 2026 
Two web design manuals 
Each 19,69 × 1,91 × 24,77 cm
The bestselling books Web Pages That Suck by Vincent Flanders and Michael 
Willis, and Son of Web Pages That Suck by Vincent Flanders and Dean Peters, 
teach web design through negative examples: good design is learned by stud-
ying “bad” websites.
With critical humour, the authors draw attention to recurring design patterns 
found on early websites—overcrowded homepages, difficult-to-read typog-
raphy, and confusing navigation. Rather than promoting rigid design dogmas, 
they emphasize simple principles such as clarity, usability, and a focus on con-
tent. Although search engines, devices, and interfaces have changed dramat-
ically since then, the underlying idea remains relevant: design fundamentally 
shapes how accessible and understandable information becomes.

My Boyfriend Came Back from the War, 1996
Website
http://www.teleportacia.org/war/
Olia Lialina’s My Boyfriend Came Back from the War is widely regarded as one 
of the most important works of early net art. Drawing on her background in film 
criticism and her first experiences creating websites, Lialina understood the in-
ternet in the 1990s not merely as a storage medium but as a distinct narrative 
medium in its own right. Her engagement with the web led her to stop writing 
film criticism and instead create films online.
Using HTML, hypertext, black-and-white bitmap images, and browser frames, 
the work tells the story of a couple reuniting after a war. By clicking hyperlinks, 
users navigate through increasingly smaller frames in which a monologue un-
folds, addressing themes of trauma, infidelity, and alienation. In doing so, Lialina 
created a new genre that she termed “net.film.”
She combined cinematic techniques such as montage with the structure of the 
web. Unlike cinema, where images unfold sequentially, the work allows images 
and temporal layers to coexist simultaneously within the browser window, cre-
ating a multilinear and open-ended narrative that cannot always be reduced 
to clear causal relationships. Although users navigate through the work, they 
cannot alter the story itself. Instead, they reveal fragments of the monologue 
and, depending on the order in which hyperlinks are clicked, create new con-
nections and narrative pathways.
6
Agatha Appears, 1997
Website, Webring 
https://www.c3.hu/collection/agatha/
Olia Lialina’s Agatha Appears is another early net art work that explores the 
internet’s potential as a storytelling medium. The story follows Agatha, a young 
girl from the countryside who encounters an unemployed system administrator 
in the city. He attempts to upload her to the internet, after which Agatha travels 
through various networks and servers until she eventually disappears and lat-
er returns online. The narrative unfolds linearly through web pages, hyperlinks, 
mouseover texts, and dialog boxes within the browser pop-ups.
Particularly significant is the close relationship between narrative and the tech-
nical structure of the web. Elements such as status and address bars, or error 
messages become part of the story, merging technical and narrative layers. 
Agatha’s journey through the internet is not only fictional but also reflects the 
actual distribution of the artwork across multiple international servers.
Despite its simple, almost childlike aesthetic and minimal interactivity, the work 
uses the possibilities of HTML in a previously unprecedented way and reflects 
on themes such as digital communication, virtual spaces, and the materiality of 
the early internet.

Online Newspapers, 2004 – ongoing  
Wall Street Journal, 2008
Webpage
The ongoing series Online Newspapers translates the visual identities of major 
newspapers into the aesthetic language of early amateur web pages. Drawing 
on the idiosyncratic, glittering interfaces of the 1990s, Olia Lialina asks what 
news websites might have looked like if they had been designed by the hobby-
ist publishers whose visual quirks shaped early web culture.
When content from newspapers such as the Frankfurter Allgemeine Zeitung,

Self-Portrait, 2018
GIF for three networks 
http://olia.lialina.work/
Self-Portrait draws attention to the different layers of the internet and makes 
visible the fact that it consists of various networks, protocols, and modes of ac-
cess beyond the regular web that usually remain hidden. In order to view the 
work in its entirety, three different browsers must be installed and opened si-
multaneously: a conventional browser such as Firefox or Chrome to view the 
regular web, the anonymous Tor Browser for access to sites hosted on the dark-
net, and the peer-to-peer browser Agregore, which displays data distributed 
via peer-to-peer file systems. Each protocol holds a different section of the art-
ist’s portrait, so that her face only emerges as a complete image through the ar-
rangement of all three browser windows. The slightly distorted image fragments 
illustrate that data travels through different networks. 
Because data is transmitted at varying speeds and browsers process and 
display content differently, the same website never appears exactly identi-
cal. Factors such as network connectivity, server load, hardware, and brows-
er technology influence when and how content becomes visible. Self-Portrait 
demonstrates that digital communication is not a uniform, stable process but 
is shaped by technical conditions that determine who sees what, when, and in 
what software. At the same time, the work makes the fragmented structure of 
the internet tangible and encourages users to exercise their own agency when 
installing and arranging different browsers on their own desktop.

Something for Everyone, 2016
Three ready-made advent calendars 
Each 18 × 24 cm  
Something for Everyone consists of three individually designed advent calen-
dars produced through a print-on-demand service. Each year, Dragan Espen-
schied, the artist’s husband, orders one calendar for each family member and 
compiles them using images found online. He selects motifs intended to evoke 
personal memories and provoke humorous reactions (LULZ).
The images are removed from their original contexts and transferred onto blank 
cardboard calendars using the customization tools provided by the online 
printing service. Created in 2016, the calendars are presented as readymades 
within an institutional art context, drawing attention to the ubiquitous availabil-
ity of digital images and their constant reinterpretation through platforms and 
niche markets.
The title references both the critique of the culture industry formulated by The-
odor Adorno and Max Horkheimer in Dialectic of Enlightenment – “Something 
is provided for everyone so that no one may escape” – and the slogan This Is for 
Everyone of the World Wide Web’s inventor Tim Berners-Lee. In doing so, the 
work brings together the promises and contradictions of digital mass culture.

Initiated in 2004, Online Newspapers, Postfactual Edition offers a fictional pre-

“commentators” within the mainstream media landscape.

Summer, 2013
Social network
http://saskia-aldinger.com/olia/summer/
Summer presents an animation of Olia Lialina swinging against a blue-and-
white gradient background. The swing is positioned as if suspended directly 
from the browser interface, while a new URL ending in “/olia/summer” appears 
in the address bar with each frame change.
The GIF is distributed across the websites of twenty-five artists and consists 
of 18 frames. Each website hosts only a single frame, yet together they form a 
seamless loop. Lialina thus “swings” across servers, domains, and geograph-
ical distances, drawing attention to the URL as a central site of the web and to 
distributed content that can be integrated into web page layouts rather than 
merely posted online.
While the artist gently swings back and forth, the web addresses flash by at 
increasing speed, transforming the movement within the browser’s address bar 
into the true stage of the work.

Diptych
<HR>, Chrome, 2014/2026
<HR>, Firefox, 2014/2026
Digital prints on paper, aluminum dibond
Each 148 × 105 × 0.3 cm 
https://art.teleportacia.org/hr.html 
<HR> is part of Olia Lialina’s Print to PDF series and takes its title from the HTML 
element “horizontal rule”, a horizontal line frequently used in the early web to 
structure and separate content. The work continues Lialina’s long-standing en-
gagement with vernacular web aesthetics, browser structures, and the visual 
language of the early Internet.
The term “vernacular web aesthetics” refers to the everyday design practices 
shaped by users during the early years of the Internet. Unlike today’s standard-
ized platforms, the web of the 1990s was characterized by individually creat-
ed websites, experimental layouts, and visible technical structures. Navigation 
bars, scroll bars, frames, and loading indicators were not merely functional ele-
ments but integral parts of the visual experience of the web.
Out of the technological possibilities and limitations of the period emerged a 
distinct visual language consisting of GIF animations, hyperlinks, icons, and 
simple HTML layouts.
By placing a seemingly inconspicuous HTML element at the centre of atten-
tion, <HR> points to these often-overlooked components of early web culture. 
The work demonstrates that even the most basic technical structures shape the 
aesthetics and perception of the internet and can nowadays be read as testi-
monies to its cultural and design history.

Hosted, 2020
Network performance, clouds and buckets 
https://hosted.z21.web.core.windows.net/
The browser performance Hosted unfolds through a long list of URLs, each 
displaying an image of the artist swimming. Through the sequence of browser 
tabs, the artist swims laps. A cinematic sequence emerges whose speed and 
stability depend on the surrounding technical environment—on clouds, hosting 
services, and buckets, the data containers that store and distribute online con-
tent. Disruptions repeatedly occur, as servers may alter or limit data, leading to 
changes in image size and appearance.
These interruptions in the flow of images point not only to different computers 
and server spaces but also to the disruptions of the cloud.

Four printed A4 sheets document the traces of an apparently simple yet un-
successful attempt to print a boarding pass for a flight. ENGWB51 makes visi-

check-in is on fragile and failure-prone technical procedures.

Give me time / This page is no more, 2015 – ongoing
160 35 mm slides, two synchronized slide projectors
Measures variable
With Give me time / This page is no more Olia Lialina created an ongoing ar-
chive of 160 websites from GeoCities.com, an early web-hosting service that 
has since been discontinued. They focus on homepages that either promise 
future completion or announce their own disappearance. Between messages 
such as “check back later” and angry declarations in which site owners sym-
bolically pronounce the death of their pages, it becomes evident how strongly 
even the early internet culture was shaped by personal emotions and individual 
expression.

Animated GIF Model, 2005
Three animated GIFs 
https://art.teleportacia.org/exhibition/AGM/
Dancing, twirling a hula hoop, or playing the accordion—Lialina herself be-
comes a GIF model inviting other web masters to use her on their web pages. 
Drawing on early web culture, in which animated GIFs were central design el-
ements of personal websites, Animated GIF Model refers to an aesthetic prac-
tice that was increasingly displaced by the rise of meme culture. Thanks to their 
transparent backgrounds, the GIFs can be copied, inserted into new contexts, 
and endlessly redistributed, serving as a simple yet expressive form of early 
internet culture.

Treasure Trove, 2017/2026
7-channel video installation
http://blingee.geocities.institute/treasure_trove/
Hundreds of glittering, overlapping GIFs dance, flicker, and swirl across the 
walls in the installation Treasure Trove. The work draws on Blingee.com, a plat-
form where users could create animated GIF collages from an apparently end-
less collection of clip art, icons, stickers, and other expressive visual elements. 
In contrast to the exclusive navigation experience of the website itself, Treasure 
Trove transforms these sparkling GIFs into an immersive 360-degree projection 
that fills the museum’s octagonal annex.
Newly arranged combinations appear alongside the username of the person

Body Class Pimp: voelmegoed, 2016/2026
Digital print on plexiglass
150 × 84 × 1 cm 
Colourful, eccentric, and visually striking profile pages characterized the Dutch 
social networking platform Hyves, which from 2004 onward offered extensive 
customization options to users. Through freely chosen backgrounds, pro-
file pictures, short text descriptions, and emojis, users created diverse visual 
self-portraits in which they consciously and creatively staged their online iden-
tities. The series Body Class Pimp preserves these individual appearances as 
networks such as Facebook standardized their design and displaced the cus-
tomizable “pimp my profile” aesthetics of platforms like Hyves.
Each work serves as a reminder that online presences were once experimental 
spaces shaped directly by users themselves, spaces for making aspects of the 
self visible through design. As competition from Facebook intensified, however, 
Hyves ceased operations in 2013.
